Rhythm

At the core of beautiful typography is rhythm. Vertical rhythm is achieved with consistent spacing of one baseline to another, regardless of font size and any other spacer elements.

Let's start with headers.

A simple h1

Despite being significantly larger, h1 headers do not break rhythm. It sits on a baseline, the same as the text around it, even if it itself occupies multiple baselines.

Just h1 over multiple lines

A short burst of large text in headers tolerate smaller leading than the main text. Descenders in one line can come close to ascenders on the next without discomfort. In most cases, and in large enough viewports, h1 headers are single lined anyway.

Scales

Related to ryhthm is judicious selection of scales: font sizes. Here, the Fibonacci sequence 2, 3, 5, 8, which approximate the Golden ratio, is used for the heading sizes. Technically it is 1, 1.5, 2.5, 4 relative to the main font, respectively for h4, h3, h2, and h1, but it's the same ratio.

Syncopations

A continuous text stream is often punctuated with non-paragraphed text. As with music, syncopations add flavour and variety. It is most important to end the syncopations by returning to the base rhythm.

Images inlined in p are right-aligned, with text flowing on the left without breaking rhythm. Images outside of p are center aligned, but we'll come back to that when we talk about caveats.

Inline styles with the same scale as the main text shouldn't break rhythm. We have many: abbr, code, definition, deleted, emphasized, inserted, keyboard, marked, small, strong, quotes, quotes in another language, or variables. As long as the height of the inlined element is not greater than the line-height, overall rhythm is not disrupted.

Lists should not break rhythm:

  • No matter what happens
  • Lists don't break vertical rhythm
  • Even when a list item is very long that it spans multiple lines; spacing must be consistent.

Responsiveness & fluidity

aside appear on the margin, or inline, depending on screen width. But always obeying vertical rhythm.

Responsive web design shows, hides, or otherwise reshuffles content according to screen size. Notice the responsive behaviour of the aside tag as you resize your browser width.

Fluidity is a lesser used technique that additionally changes font sizes based on screen size. It exploits the fact that users generally hold smaller screens closer, and don't need as large fonts. Fluidity smooths out the differences between similar screen sizes, ensuring more consistent measure and thus design.

A measure of 49 to 75 characters per line is generally desirable, with 66 characters per line ideal. Fluidity ensures that on large screens, text width is capped to ensure a good maximum measure; and on small screens, the reduced root font size ensures that the minimum measure is kept for as long as possible. Ultimately on very small screens, the desired minimum measure is unattainable, and we resort to just keeping a decent font size.

Caveats

Ultimately HTML and CSS, is not InDesign or Quark. We lack the fine control that those programs offer. With a few hacks with baselines and padding, we've already achieved a fair amount. But there are some more elements that don't quite work.

Images & other media

Use art direction to scale images according to screen size, but that's a different topic.

This one is particularly obvious. Anything non-text plays by its own rules, and without complicated Javascript or other CSS trickery to add the necessary screen size dependent padding, we're not likely able to bring the subsequent text back inline.

Form elements also tend to use browser native widgets to provide the native look and feel. As such, they are likely to disrupt rhythm. Worst is the textarea, which users can resize on-demand. What follows may or may not line up, depending on your browser.

Font changes

Since CSS doesn't follow the more conventional notion of a baseline, which is fixed regardless of font, changing fonts is problematic and causes loss of vertical rhythm.

Any inline elements that enlarge height is particularly disruptive, as it alters the effective line-height just for the line it is occupying. And there is no way to control it. Use things like xsub = ysup with caution. Preferably, don't inline them, as the lines before and after them look strange with inconsistent spacing - you get one tall line surrounded by normal ones.

Conclusion

Maintaining perfect vertical rhythm throughout the entire page may not be that important considering the vertical scrolling nature of HTML. At any one scroll position, the visible page can still mostly be in rhythm. Nonetheless, the other topics presented here can lead to beautiful typography using just CSS.

Text on a footer is usually brief and smaller than the main font. Just like music, consistent rhythm fades away at the end, bringing the piece to an end.
